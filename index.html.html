Method & Path

Trajectory

Track & field has been part of my life since age 7. A forced stop during a critical growth period removed me from competition, but it also reset my approach to training and progression. When I returned at 15, I made a clear decision to specialize entirely in sprint — 60m, 100m, and 200m — with a structured performance focus.

In 2025, I sustained an avulsion fracture of the anterior tibial tuberosity. Two screws held the repair for twelve months. Hardware is now removed and return-to-sport is active — competition planned for Fall 2026. This injury fundamentally changed the way I see progression and my own body, forcing me to shift from reactive training to structured, goal-oriented development.

The enforced pause was not empty. I independently studied sprint biomechanics and strength development principles, analyzed elite sprint mechanics through video breakdowns, and built a structured return-to-training framework combining rehab progression, strength planning, and sprint reintroduction logic. I also maintained a personal tracking system for pain, range of motion, strength, and recovery variables to guide decision-making over time.

Performance is not built on intensity. It is built on structure executed consistently over time. Six interdependent pillars. Not complex — consistent.

Velocity

Neural speed expression is trained, not forced. Speed is refined through quality, not fatigue.

Strength

Force is only relevant if it improves sprint mechanics and transfer under velocity.

Technique

Technical efficiency defines how force is expressed at speed — not how much force is produced.

Mental

My mental approach is not driven by motivation but by structure. Consistency and standards maintained without external validation — especially during long periods away from competition.

Recovery

Recovery is a performance variable, managed with the same structure as training load and intensity.

Nutrition

Nutrition is structured to fuel training demands and recovery capacity, with precise adjustment based on load, phase of training, and adaptation needs.
